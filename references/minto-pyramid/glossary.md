# Glossary — The Minto Pyramid Principle

**Blank assertion (intellectually blank assertion)** — a summary point that only states the *count* or *category* of the ideas below it ("the company has five problems"), giving the reader no insight (Ch 7).

**Bottom-up build** — building the pyramid by listing points, working out their relationships, and drawing the conclusion; used when the Main Point isn't yet clear (Ch 3).

**Complication** — what happened in the Situation to raise the reader's Question; the "C" of SCQA (Ch 2, 4, 8).

**Deductive reasoning** — a grouping whose points derive from each other in successive steps to a "therefore"; summarized resting on the final point (Ch 5).

**Degree order (comparative order / order of importance)** — ordering a classified group by degree, most important first (Ch 6).

**Desired Result (R2)** — the specific, quantifiable result the reader wants in place of R1 (Ch 8).

**Diagnostic framework** — a visualization of the problem area (physical structure, cause-effect tree, or MECE classification) that reveals the likely causes to analyze (Ch 9).

**Disturbing Event** — what threatens the stable Opening Scene and triggers R1; External, Internal, or Recently Recognized (Ch 8).

**Exhibit slide** — a chart, graph, table, or diagram that conveys data or structure; distinct from a text slide (Ch 11).

**Hierarchical headings** — Minto's preferred device for showing the pyramid on the page; increasingly subordinate ideas placed further right (Ch 10).

**Horizontal relationship** — the logic *within* a grouping; always deductive or inductive (Ch 2).

**Idea** — a statement that raises a question in the reader's mind because it tells them something they don't know (Ch 2).

**Inductive leap** — the inference drawn from a same-kind group; the insight that becomes the point above (Ch 5, 7).

**Inductive reasoning** — a grouping of same-kind ideas (nameable by one plural noun) yielding an inference about their sameness (Ch 5).

**Key Line** — the top row of answers directly beneath the Main Point; the document's spine (Ch 2, 3).

**Level of abstraction** — a layer of the pyramid; each layer down is more specific, each up is a summary (Ch 1, 2).

**Logic tree** — a MECE, *nondiagnostic* tree used to generate and check recommendations; contrast with a diagnostic framework (Ch 9).

**Main Point** — the single governing sentence at the apex of the pyramid; the answer the whole document delivers. (Some secondary literature calls this the "governing thought"; Minto's term is Main Point.) (Ch 1, 3).

**MECE** — Minto's coinage: **M**utually **E**xclusive, **C**ollectively **E**xhaustive; the test that a division has no overlaps and no gaps (Ch 6).

**Memory image** — the mental picture the reader builds piece by piece while reading; the thing clear prose lets them reconstruct (Ch 12).

**Opening Scene / Starting Point** — the stable existing structure or process where a problem originated (Ch 8).

**Plural noun** — the single noun that names an inductive group (reasons, steps, problems); if you can't name the group with one, it isn't valid (Ch 5, 6).

**Problem-Definition Framework** — Situation (Opening Scene + Disturbing Event) → R1 → R2; converts directly into an SCQA introduction (Ch 8).

**Pyramid Principle** — structure every document so its ideas form a pyramid under a single Main Point, delivered top-down (Ch 1).

**Question** — the single question the document answers, identified by the introduction (Ch 2, 3, 4).

**Remind, not inform** — the introduction's rule: include only what the reader already knows or will accept; no proof/exhibits (Ch 4).

**SCQA** — Situation, Complication, Question, Answer; the narrative flow of the introduction (Ch 2, 4).

**Sequential Analysis** — the five problem-solving questions: is there a problem / where / why / what could we do / what should we do (Ch 8).

**Situation** — the noncontroversial setup the reader agrees is true; the "S" of SCQA (Ch 2, 4, 8).

**"So what?"** — the reader's prompt (imagined) that surfaces the Complication during the top-down build (Ch 3).

**Storyboard** — the plan mapping the pyramid to a sequence of slides; each frame's message written across the top (Ch 11).

**Structural order** — ordering a group by the structure of the whole it divides; must be MECE (Ch 6).

**Text slide** — a slide carrying only the most significant ideas (the pyramid's major points), stark and simple (Ch 11).

**Time order** — ordering a group as steps or a cause-and-effect sequence: first, second, third (Ch 6).

**Top-down build** — building the pyramid from the apex using the introductory flow (the box procedure); the easier, preferred method (Ch 3).

**Undesired Result (R1)** — the problem the reader faces; the gap from R2 that defines the problem (Ch 8).

**Vertical relationship** — the question/answer dialogue *between* pyramid levels (Ch 2).
