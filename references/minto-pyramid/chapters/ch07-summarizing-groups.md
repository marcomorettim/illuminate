# Chapter 7: Summarizing Grouped Ideas

## Core Idea
The hardest pyramid rule: the point *above* a grouping must state the **insight the group yields** — the inductive leap — not a placeholder. After drafting, most writers are guilty of two errors: lists justified only by a shared plural noun, and topping them with an **intellectually blank assertion**.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Kill the blank assertion.** "The company has five problems," "We have three objectives," "There are four steps" — these tell the reader nothing and waste the summary slot. The point above must carry a *revealing insight*.
- **How to find the real summary point:**
  - For a group of **actions** (steps, recommendations): state the **effect** of carrying them out — the result they jointly produce.
  - For a group of **situations / conclusions**: state the **implication** — the inference about what is the same across them (the inductive leap).
- **The rewording technique**: rewrite each point as a full sentence, then look hard at what is genuinely alike about them, and say *that*. A real summary point **inspires further thinking**; a blank one stops it.

## Key Concepts
- **Intellectually blank assertion** — a summary that only announces the *count* or *category* of what's below ("five problems"), not its meaning.
- **Inductive leap** — the inference you draw from a same-kind group; the insight that becomes the point above.
- **Effect (of actions) vs. implication (of situations)** — the two shapes a real summary takes.

## Mental Models
- A grouping is a promise: the point above owes the reader the *So what?* of the points below. Blank assertions default on that promise.
- If summarizing your group produces only "there are N of them," you haven't thought the group through — the members probably share a deeper structure (often cause-and-effect) you haven't surfaced.

## Anti-patterns
- **Lists matched only by the plural noun** — "ten steps," "five problems" grouped because they fit the label, not because they share an internal logic.
- **Topping a list with its own headcount** instead of its meaning.

## Worked Example
Given "The company has five problems," Minto's move is to reword each of the five as a full sentence and ask what's the same. Doing so reveals they are not five independent problems at all — each is an *effect* of one underlying cause (for instance, a refusal to delegate authority). The honest summary point is therefore that single cause, with the five former "problems" re-sorted beneath it as its consequences. The blank count "five problems" is replaced by a point that actually advances the reader's understanding — and reshapes the pyramid in the process.

## Key Takeaways
1. Never let a summary point merely count or categorize what's below it.
2. Actions → state their combined effect; situations → state their implication.
3. Reword-to-full-sentences is the mechanical way to expose the real, shared point.
