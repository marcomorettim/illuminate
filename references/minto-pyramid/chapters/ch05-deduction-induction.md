# Chapter 5: Deduction and Induction — The Difference

## Core Idea
Deduction and induction are the *only* two ways to relate a grouping of ideas horizontally. Deduction is a line of reasoning to a "therefore"; induction is a set of same-kind ideas yielding an inference. **Deductive points derive from each other; inductive points don't** — and while the mind reasons deductively, induction is usually the clearer way to *write*.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Deductive reasoning — three moves** (Minto drops the "major/minor premise" jargon as confusing):
  1. Make a statement about a situation that exists in the world.
  2. Make another statement about a *related* situation existing at the same time — it relates by commenting on the **subject or the predicate** of the first.
  3. State the **implication** of both existing at once.
  - The point above summarizes it, **resting heavily on the final point**.
- **Chaining**: you may skip steps and chain deductive arguments *if the reader will grasp and agree with the missing steps*. But a chained deductive argument never runs beyond ~4 points.
- **Inductive reasoning**: take ideas that are the same kind of thing (describable by one **plural noun** — reasons, steps, problems), then draw an inference about their sameness. An inductive grouping never runs beyond ~5 points.
- **Why prefer induction in writing**: deduction makes the reader climb up and down the reasoning ladder to hold your point; induction lets them see the whole group at once. Deductive is "a useful way to think, a ponderous way to write."

## Key Concepts
- **Derive from each other** (deductive) vs. **alike but independent** (inductive) — the acid test.
- **Plural noun** — the name of an inductive group; if you can't name it with one, it isn't a valid group.
- **Rest on the final point** — how a deductive group is summarized.

## Mental Models
- To summarize a **deduction**, paraphrase the "therefore." To summarize an **induction**, state what's the same about the members.
- If your support points *comment on each other in sequence*, you're deductive; if they're parallel members of a class, you're inductive.

## Anti-patterns
- **Deduction where induction would read better** — forcing the reader up a ladder for a point better shown as a group.
- **A "group" you can't name with a single plural noun** — it isn't a real inductive group; the ideas don't belong together yet.

## Worked Example
Deduction — Minto's Socrates argument: "Men are mortal" (a situation in the world), "Socrates is a man" (a related situation commenting on the subject), "Therefore Socrates is mortal" (the implication) — summarized resting on the last point: *"Because Socrates is a man he is mortal."* Induction — the tanks: "French tanks are at the Polish border," "German tanks are at the Polish border," "Russian tanks are at the Polish border." These don't derive from one another; they're the same kind of thing (warlike movements against Poland), so you draw the inference: *"Poland is about to be invaded by tanks."*

## Key Takeaways
1. Every grouping is deductive or inductive — decide and don't blend.
2. Deductive summary rests on the "therefore"; inductive summary names the sameness.
3. When you can, convert ponderous deduction into a clean inductive group for the reader.
