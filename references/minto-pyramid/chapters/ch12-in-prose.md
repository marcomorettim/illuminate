# Chapter 12: Reflecting the Pyramid in Prose

## Core Idea
Writing clearly is two steps: **decide the point, then put it into words.** Once the pyramid is built you already have the points and their order — all that remains is the wording. The technique that makes wording clear: **we think in images, so "see" the idea first, then copy that image into words** — and the reader re-creates the image from your words.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Create the image, then copy it in words.** Consciously visualize the image you used when you first thought up the idea; transcribe *that*. Concrete images produce concrete prose; the reader rebuilds the picture and both understands and enjoys it.
- **Find the image lurking in bad prose.** To fix dehydrated, jargon-heavy writing, recover the picture the writer must have had in mind, then rewrite from the picture rather than editing the words.
- **The two-step discipline restated**: because thinking is done in images that compress "seven or eight" facts into one configuration, the writer's job is to unpack the right image into sentences — not to dress ideas in technical fashion.

## Key Concepts
- **Memory image** — the picture the reader builds piecemeal while reading (grows detail by detail).
- **See-it-then-say-it** — visualize before you write a sentence.
- **Dehydrated prose** — long-winded, jargon-loaded writing that hides a simple image.

## Mental Models
- "Good ideas ought not to be dressed up in bad prose." Technical subjects can still be works of literary art (James, Freud, Whitehead, Russell).
- If a sentence won't come clear, you've lost the image — stop editing words and re-picture what you're actually describing.

## Anti-patterns
- **Jargon as respectability** — believing the more dehydrated and technical the style, the more respect it commands. It's fashion, not necessity.
- **Editing bad prose word-by-word** instead of recovering and rewriting from the underlying image.

## Worked Example
Minto sets a jargon sentence — *"A primary area of potential improvement is improving cost-effectiveness of field sales-force deployment… dictated by changes in the trade environment"* — against a passage from Thoreau's *Walden*: *"Near the end of March 1845 I borrowed an axe and went down to the woods by Walden Pond… and began to cut down some tall, arrowy white pines…"* Reading Thoreau, you build a **memory image** piece by piece — a gray March day, one figure borrowing an axe, walking to the woods, white pines on a hillside, the ice-covered pond — because the writer saw it and copied the image into words. The jargon sentence offers no image to build, so it stays impenetrable. The fix for the latter is to recover its picture and write *that*.

## Key Takeaways
1. Once the pyramid is built, wording is the only task left — don't re-litigate structure in prose.
2. Visualize the idea, then copy the image into words; the reader reconstructs it.
3. Repair bad prose by recovering its lost image, not by polishing its jargon.
