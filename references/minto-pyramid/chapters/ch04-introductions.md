# Chapter 4: Fine Points of Introductions

## Core Idea
The introduction exists to **remind, not to inform**: it tells the reader (in story form) only what they already know or would readily accept, so as to raise — not prove — the Question your document answers. There is always exactly **one** beginning Question.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The three rules of a good introduction:**
  1. **Remind rather than inform** — include nothing that would have to be *proved* for the reader to accept your points. No exhibits in the introduction.
  2. **Always contain the three story elements** — Situation, Complication, and Solution (add "what's to come" in long documents); woven into story form, not necessarily in classic order.
  3. **Length follows the reader's need** — include whatever is required for full understanding (history, your involvement, prior investigations, definitions), all woven into the story.
- **The four questions a document answers**: (1) What should we do? (2) How should we/did we do it? (3) Should we do it? (4) Why did it happen?
- **The four common business patterns:**
  - **Giving Direction** — you *plant* the question. S: we want to do X; C: need you to do Y; Q: How do I do Y? (Q usually implied — but spell it out for yourself.)
  - **Seeking Approval to Spend Money** — S: we have a problem; C: we have a solution costing $X; Q: Should I approve? Defended by 3–4 standard reasons: resolution can't wait / this solves it (or is the best option) / cost is offset by savings / other goodies.
  - **Explaining "How to"** — Key Line is always **Steps**; lay out the present process and the recommended process — the *differences* dictate the steps.
  - **Choosing Among Alternatives** — alternatives always go in the **Complication** (they must be known to the reader in advance); structure the Key Line around the *criteria* used to choose.

## Key Concepts
- **Remind-not-inform** — the introduction's governing constraint.
- **The single beginning Question** — two questions must reduce to one ("Should we enter, and if so how?" is really "How should we enter?").
- **Plant vs. remind** — directives plant a question the reader didn't have; most patterns remind one they did.

## Mental Models
- A good introduction does more than hold interest — it *frames* interpretation, "in much the same way that a trial lawyer's opening statement seeks to give the jury a framework." Use that power honestly.
- If you can't find the Question from the intro, look at the points you want to make and work backward: they answer *some* question; what situation would have raised it?

## Anti-patterns
- **Proof in the introduction** (exhibits, arguments that must be defended) — it belongs in the body.
- **Bringing up alternatives to knock them down** ("three ways, A is bad, B is bad, therefore C") — you choose C because it *solves the problem*, not because A and B are bad.

## Worked Example
A request for funds, structured to remind: **S** — "work in our department has increased 20% a year for four years, yet per headquarters policy we've held head count to 14 people; the result is overtime, weekend work, and a growing backlog." **C** — "the backlog has reached 22 weeks, which the field finds unacceptable, and we have no scope for more hours; research shows an IBM machine would cut both the backlog and overtime." **Q** (implied) — Should I approve? **A** — "We urge approval." Every line is something the reader already knows or will readily accept; nothing is *proved* — the proof waits for the body.

## Key Takeaways
1. The introduction narrates the origin of one Question; it never argues.
2. Learn the four patterns — they cover the overwhelming majority of business writing and give you an instant scaffold.
3. For "how to," make both processes visible before writing; the gap *is* your Key Line.
