# Chapter 1: Why a Pyramid Structure?

## Core Idea
The mind automatically sorts information into pyramidal groupings to comprehend it, so any document is easiest to absorb when its ideas are pre-sorted into a pyramid and delivered **top-down** — the single main point first, supporting ideas below.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Pyramid Principle**: every document should be deliberately structured so its ideas form a pyramid under a single governing point (Minto's term is the **Main Point**, not "governing thought").
  - When to use: any time you must communicate more than a couple of ideas in writing or speech.
  - How: put the one message the reader must leave with at the apex; group everything else beneath it so each layer explains or defends the layer above.
- **The three rules that make it a pyramid** (tested *before* you write):
  1. Ideas at any level must **summarize** the ideas grouped below them.
  2. Ideas in each grouping must be **the same kind of idea** (nameable by one plural noun).
  3. Ideas in each grouping must be **logically ordered** — a specific reason the second comes second.
  - A broken rule signals a flaw in the thinking, not just the writing.

## Key Concepts
- **Main Point** — the single sentence at the apex; the answer the whole document delivers.
- **Level of abstraction** — each layer down is more specific; each layer up is a summary.
- **Pre-sorting** — doing the grouping *for* the reader so their mind doesn't have to.

## Mental Models
- Think of the reader as facing ~100 sentences they must take in, relate, and hold together. A pyramid does that work for them in advance.
- Use the mind's own habit: it imposes order on anything that occurs together (the Greeks saw constellations in scattered stars). Give it order deliberately rather than letting it invent its own.

## Anti-patterns
- **Bottom-up delivery**: making the reader wade through findings and assemble the point themselves. Order of writing (bottom-up discovery) is not order of presentation (top-down).
- **Mixed-level groupings**: a "group" whose members sit at different levels of abstraction — a sign the thinking isn't sorted yet.

## Worked Example
Minto demonstrates the value of grouping with a memory test. She lists unrelated noun pairs — LAKE·SUGAR, BOOT·PLATE, PENCIL·GASOLINE, RAILROAD·ELEPHANT — and asks you to picture each pair in a shared situation (sugar dissolving in the lake, the boot on the plate). Cover the right column, read only the left, and most people recall every pair without hesitation. The imposed logical unit makes disconnected items retainable — exactly what a pyramid does for a document's ideas.

## Key Takeaways
1. Structure is not decoration; the reader's mind *demands* a pyramid and will invent a worse one if you don't supply a better one.
2. Decide the pyramid before writing — the rules catch flawed thinking cheaply, before rewriting is expensive.
3. Lead with the answer. Suspense is for mysteries, not business documents.
