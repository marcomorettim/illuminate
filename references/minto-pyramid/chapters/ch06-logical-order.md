# Chapter 6: Imposing Logical Order

## Core Idea
Every inductive grouping must have a reason its members sit in the order they do. There are only **three possible orders**, because there are only three analytical activities that create a grouping: divide a whole, trace cause and effect, or classify. The order you use *reveals* which activity formed the group — and checking it catches flaws in the thinking.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The three orders** (each tied to the activity that produced the group):
  - **Time Order** — from *determining the causes of an effect* / laying out a process: first, second, third. Use whenever the points are steps or a cause-and-effect sequence.
  - **Structural Order** — from *dividing a whole into its parts*: the order the structure itself dictates (e.g., Boston/New York/Washington; front-to-back of a process; org chart). Every division here must be **MECE**.
  - **Degree Order** — from *classifying like things*: rank by degree, i.e., order of importance (most important first). Also called comparative order.
- **MECE** — Minto's own coinage (she "abbreviates this mouthful"): **Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive**. A division that overlaps or leaves gaps is not yet valid.

## Key Concepts
- **Time / Structural / Degree** — the three (and only three) valid orders.
- **MECE** — the test that a structural division is clean and complete.
- **Order reveals process** — the order you can defend tells you (and the reader) how you thought.

## Mental Models
- If you can't say *why* point two comes second, you haven't finished thinking — you've listed, not ordered.
- To sort a flat list, ask: did I get these by dividing, by tracing cause-and-effect, or by classifying? The answer names the order.

## Anti-patterns
- **Failing to distinguish cause from effect** — the most common error; listing items as siblings when some are *causes of the others* (which belong a level down, in time order).
- **Not recognizing a process** — treating steps of one procedure as an unordered "list."

## Worked Example
Minto takes a flat "problems" list a manager wrote and shows the fix. Reworded and examined, several items turn out to be *effects* of others — e.g., each is really the result of a refusal to delegate authority. Once you see the cause-and-effect relationships, the list re-sorts: the causes rise, the effects drop beneath them in **time order**, and the true structure emerges. The trick she prescribes: **visualize yourself actually taking the actions or living the process**, which exposes which items cause which. Structural order she illustrates as MECE division — describing the pieces of a whole in the sequence the structure dictates.

## Key Takeaways
1. Order is meaning: the only valid orders are time, structural, and degree.
2. Every structural division must pass the MECE test — no overlaps, no gaps.
3. Distinguish cause from effect before ordering; effects belong under their causes.
