# Chapter 2: The Substructures Within the Pyramid

## Core Idea
Because you rarely know your ideas precisely before writing, the pyramid gives you three substructures that *speed discovery*: the vertical Q&A relationship between levels, the horizontal logic within a grouping, and the narrative flow of the introduction.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The vertical relationship — a question/answer dialogue.** Every idea is *a statement that raises a question in the reader's mind* (you tell them something they don't know → they ask Why? / How? / Why do you say that?). You answer horizontally on the line below, which raises further questions, until the reader has none left. Then return to the **Key Line** and continue.
  - When to use: to find what each box below must contain — whatever *answers the question the box above provokes*.
- **The horizontal relationship — deductive OR inductive, never both.** A grouping answers its question *logically* in exactly one of two ways:
  - **Deductive**: successive steps — a statement about the world, a second commenting on its subject or predicate, then the "therefore." Summarized by resting on the final point.
  - **Inductive**: a set of ideas of the same kind (nameable by a plural noun) → an inference about their sameness.
- **The introductory flow — SCQA.** The introduction traces the history of the reader's question as a story: **Situation → Complication → Question → Answer**, so writer and reader are "standing in the same place" before the reasoning begins.

## Key Concepts
- **Idea** — a statement that raises a question (not merely a fact).
- **Key Line** — the top row of answers directly under the Main Point; the document's spine.
- **Vertical / horizontal** — vertical = between levels (Q&A); horizontal = within a level (logic).

## Mental Models
- Hold the reader's attention by controlling the dialogue: never raise a question before you're ready to answer it, and never answer one you haven't raised.
- The introduction *identifies* the question; the vertical dialogue *answers* it; the horizontal logic makes each answer valid.

## Anti-patterns
- **An "Our Assumptions" (or "Background") section before the major points** — it answers questions the reader hasn't had the chance to raise, so the information must be re-read later at the point it's actually needed.
- **Mixing deduction and induction** in one grouping — the reader can't tell how the ideas relate.

## Worked Example
Minto uses G. K. Chesterton's argument that pigs should be kept as pets to show the vertical dialogue. Chesterton: pigs should be pets. *Reader: Why?* "Two reasons: they're extremely beautiful, and they could be bred to fascinating variations." *Reader: What makes pigs beautiful?* "They're marvelously fat and typically English." *Reader: What's beautiful about being fat?* "It presents lovely curves to the onlooker and creates modesty in the possessor." At each step the reader knows exactly *why* the claim is made — even while disagreeing — because every grouping does its one job: answering the question raised above. (She then shows the identical structure in a 20-page memo recommending a British Leyland franchise: "a good buy for three reasons," each reason answering the next question down.)

## Key Takeaways
1. You don't need to know your ideas first — the substructures help you *discover* them.
2. Every box's content is dictated by the question its parent raises; if a point doesn't answer that question, it doesn't belong.
3. Pick deductive or inductive for each grouping and commit.
