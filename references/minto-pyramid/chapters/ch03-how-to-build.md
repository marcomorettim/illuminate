# Chapter 3: How to Build a Pyramid Structure

## Core Idea
You know more about your end product than you think (there will be one top sentence, answering one reader question arising from a Situation + Complication). Use that to build **top-down** where you can — it's easier — and **bottom-up** only when the top isn't yet clear.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The top-down approach** (the box procedure — Minto's Exhibit 4). Use the introductory flow to pull points out of your head one at a time:
  1. **Draw the top box.** Write the **Subject** you're discussing.
  2. **Decide the Question** — visualize the reader; what do you want answered in their mind about the Subject?
  3. **Write the Answer** (or note that you can answer it).
  4. **Identify the Situation** — the first noncontroversial statement about the Subject the reader will agree is true.
  5. **Develop the Complication** — imagine the reader nodding "Yes, I know that, **so what?**"; what happened in that Situation to raise the Question?
  6. **Recheck Question & Answer** — the Complication should trigger exactly the Question you wrote; if not, fix the Complication, Question, or Answer.
  7. **Structure the Key Line** — the Answer raises a New Question; answer it deductively or inductively.
- **The bottom-up approach** (when you can't state the top): list every point you want to make, work out the relationships between them, then draw the conclusion the group implies — and restructure into a pyramid.

## Key Concepts
- **Subject / Question / Answer** — the three things in the top box.
- **Situation / Complication** — the noncontroversial setup and the thing that went wrong.
- **"So what?"** — the reader's prompt that surfaces the Complication.

## Mental Models
- The whole exercise exists to pin down **the one Question you're answering**. Once you have the Question, everything else falls into place.
- Don't start drafting the introduction's prose; use its *structure* as a set of tongs to extract the right points.

## Anti-patterns
- **Points that don't answer the question**: the classic failure — a memo that lists what the writer did or found instead of answering what the reader asked.
- **Writing the intro paragraph first** instead of filling the boxes first.

## Worked Example
Minto rewrites a real memo from the Accounting Department of a soft-drinks company. A customer ("Big Chief") receives many deliveries and proposes recording delivery tickets on a computer disk and sending disk + check monthly — collapsing the billing cycle from a five-step, five-week process (process tickets → send bill → receive check → process payment) to a one-day one (receive disk & check → process payment). The original memo answers "here's our review and our findings are as follows…" — a list that never answers the reader's actual question (*Is the change feasible, and should we do it?*). Running it through the box procedure surfaces the Situation (current billing process), the Complication (customer's proposal), and the real Question — and the points reorganize to answer it.

## Key Takeaways
1. Prefer top-down; try bottom-up only when the apex is genuinely unknown.
2. The Situation is what the reader already agrees is true; the Complication is what makes them ask the Question.
3. If your points don't answer the Question, you have the wrong points — or the wrong Question.
