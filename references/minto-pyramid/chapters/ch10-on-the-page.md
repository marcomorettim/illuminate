# Chapter 10: Reflecting the Pyramid on the Page

## Core Idea
The reader's eye sees the logic before the mind comprehends it, so lay out the page to make the pyramid's hierarchy visible — ideally the reader absorbs your **Introduction, Main Point, and Key Line in the first 30 seconds**. Use what the eye sees to reinforce what the mind receives.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Five devices to reflect the hierarchy**: (a) hierarchical headings *(Minto's preference)*, (b) numbered & underlined points, (c) decimal numbering, (d) indented display, (e) dot-dash outlines. The format must match the levels of abstraction in the argument.
- **Six rules for hierarchical headings:**
  1. **Never use only one of any element** — a heading marks *one of a group*; you can't have a single subsection or dash point.
  2. **Show parallel ideas in parallel form** — same grammatical form for members of a group (all verbs, or all "-ing," etc.); parallelism holds *within* a group, not across groups.
  3. **Limit wording to the essence** — headings remind, they don't dominate; keep them concise.
  4. **Regard headings as outside the text** — they're for the eye; the document must read as a smooth whole *without* them (don't let a heading carry your message).
  5. **Introduce each group of headings** — state the point the group supports and the ideas to come, or you hand the reader a mystery story.
  6. **Don't overdo** — the most important rule; use headings only where they genuinely clarify.
- **Short documents**: if each Key Line point has only a paragraph or two, just **underline** the points so they "jump out."

## Key Concepts
- **The 30-second rule** — Introduction + Main Point + Key Line visible almost immediately.
- **Headings are for the eye** — navigation, not narration.
- **ToC as precis** — proper headings make the table of contents a summary of the report.

## Mental Models
- A good table of contents is a *precis of your thinking* — but only if the boxes hold real ideas. This works only because you put real points in the pyramid.
- Never place a section heading immediately after the title, or a subsection heading immediately after a section heading — introduce the group first.

## Anti-patterns
- **Magazine-style headings** stuck in to break up the page — a heading must signal *one of a group*, not decorate.
- **A ToC of empty labels** — Introduction / Background / Findings / Conclusions / Recommendations. These are process labels, not divisions of thought; "Background" and "Introduction" even overlap.

## Worked Example
The "Superfight Sweepstakes" memo shows the short-document technique: three concerns are each stated as a numbered, underlined lead — *"How will viewers learn the rules?"*, *"Will they do the forecast?"*, *"Will the commercial be clear?"* — so they literally jump out at the eye, and the reader grasps the structure at a glance. For a longer document Minto shows parallel-form headings: **Appoint a Full-Time Chief Executive** (sub-points "To Coordinate Activities," "To Effect Improvements") and **Establish Clear Lines of Authority** (sub-points "Regrouping Hotels…," "Assigning Responsibility…") — each group internally parallel, the two groups free to differ.

## Key Takeaways
1. The layout must *show* the pyramid; the eye leads the mind.
2. Every heading marks one member of a group — never a lone element, always in parallel form.
3. If your ToC reads "Findings / Conclusions / Recommendations," your boxes hold labels, not ideas.
