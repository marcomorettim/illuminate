# Chapter 11: Reflecting the Pyramid on the Screen

## Core Idea
A live presentation is a **show** — it needs a star, a script, and a **storyboard**. Translate the pyramid into clean **text slides** (the significant ideas) supported by **exhibit slides** (data and structure), each conveying its message as **starkly and simply as possible**.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Text slides** carry only the **most significant ideas** — the pyramid's major points, not everything:
  - One message per slide.
  - **Keep numbers simple** — "$4.9 million" is easier to grasp than "$4,876,321."
  - Limit to emphasizing major points; a run of look-alike text slides gets boring, so design each to be **visually interesting** (treat it as an exhibit).
- **Exhibit slides** (charts, graphs, tables, diagrams) use a wholly different means to convey the message — generally showing the **parts of a structure or process**, or displaying data — and must be **simple and readable**.
- **Storyboard → script**: move from the pyramid to a storyboard (a blank form with the *message* written across the top of each frame) to a script. This forces the pyramid's logic into the sequence of slides.
- **Slides reflect the pyramid**: a slide to clarify the structure of the presentation, slides for each major point, a slide for recommendations / next steps.

## Key Concepts
- **Text slide vs. exhibit slide** — words carrying the argument vs. graphics carrying the data.
- **Storyboard** — the plan that maps pyramid → slide sequence.
- **One message per slide** — the discipline that keeps slides from becoming documents.

## Mental Models
- You're producing a *show*, not projecting a report — the pyramid supplies the script; the slides emphasize, they don't reproduce.
- If a text slide needs a paragraph, it's a document page in disguise — cut it to its major point and let the commentary carry the rest.

## Anti-patterns
- **Cramming** full prose or dense numbers onto slides — the audience reads instead of listening.
- **Slides that don't map to the pyramid** — decoration that hides rather than reveals the structure.

## Worked Example
Minto (crediting Gene Zelazny's rules) shows the pyramid becoming a slide sequence: an opening **text slide to clarify the structure** of the talk, a **text slide per major (Key Line) point** stated starkly, **exhibit slides** underneath each to show the supporting data or structure, and a closing **text slide of recommendations / next steps** — all planned first on a storyboard whose top line states each frame's single message. Numbers on the exhibits are rounded ("$4.9 million") so the eye grasps them instantly.

## Key Takeaways
1. Text slides = the pyramid's major points; exhibit slides = the data — keep both stark.
2. Storyboard the whole show before making a single slide.
3. One message per slide; round the numbers; make each slide worth looking at.
