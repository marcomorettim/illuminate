# Chapter 9: Structuring the Analysis of the Problem

## Core Idea
Don't "go after everything." **Visualize the problem area with a diagnostic framework, derive the likely causes as hypotheses, then gather data only to prove each yes or no.** Hypothesis-first analysis is what separates efficient problem-solving from the industry norm where a large share of fact-finding is wasted.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hypothesis-driven analysis**: get the "likely possible reasons" not from thin air but by **looking critically at the structure of the area where the problem occurred** (the Opening Scene). Then design **yes/no questions** and collect data to confirm or refute — not to accumulate "interesting facts."
- **Diagnostic frameworks** (to find *causes*) — three ways, mirroring the three orders of Ch 6:
  1. **Show the physical structure** — draw the system's units/flow; each becomes a yes/no question. (Is share down because we don't make the customer *aware*, don't get *trial*, or don't win *repurchase*?)
  2. **Trace cause and effect** — build a tree of financial elements, tasks, or activities: e.g., a **ROI/financial-structure tree**, an **EPS tree decomposed into managerial tasks** (Increase Net Sales, Reduce Leaf Cost…), or an **activity tree** tracing what produces an undesired result.
  3. **Classify possible causes** — a **MECE classification** of causes, assessed in order of ease of elimination.
- **Diagnostic frameworks vs. logic trees**: a diagnostic framework *analyzes* (finds causes); a **logic tree** is *nondiagnostic* and *generates/checks recommendations*. They're often lumped as "Issue Analysis" — keep them distinct so you use the right one.
- Every breakdown must be **MECE**, and every node should resolve to a **yes/no question** you can answer with data.

## Key Concepts
- **Diagnostic framework** — a visualization of the problem area that reveals what to analyze.
- **Logic tree** — a MECE tree for generating and validating recommendations.
- **Hypothesis / yes-no question** — the unit of efficient analysis.

## Mental Models
- Assess candidate causes **in order of ease of elimination** — you don't book a brain-tumor scan before ruling out a sinus headache.
- Your definitions of Opening Scene, Disturbing Event, R1, and R2 **may change** as data comes in — refine them; the relationships between the parts stay stable.

## Anti-patterns
- **Grouping raw facts** under topic headings (Operations, Marketing, Growth) or process labels (**Findings, Conclusions, Recommendations**) — these force no thinking and yield the blank-assertion problem of Ch 7.
- **"Go after everything"** to be safe — expensive, and it buries the real problem in marginally-relevant exhibits.

## Worked Example
The headache diagnostic (from an internal Andersen presentation): *why does my head hurt, and how do I treat it?* A **MECE classification** splits the cause into **physical** or **mental**. If physical, the subcause is **external** (bumped it, allergies, weather…) or **internal**. If mental, **stress/tension** or **hypochondria**. Laid out this way, you test the possibilities **in order of ease of elimination** rather than jumping to the worst case. Minto pairs this with a **physical-structure** framework for a retail share problem — drawing the consumer path **Awareness → Trial → Repurchase** across wholesale and retail, with the retailer's levers (shelving, pricing, special-merchandise support) — so each element becomes a yes/no question about *why* share (R1) is down.

## Key Takeaways
1. Structure the analysis before gathering data; hypotheses first, facts second.
2. Pick the diagnostic method that fits — physical structure, cause-effect tree, or MECE classification.
3. Distinguish diagnostic frameworks (find causes) from logic trees (make recommendations).
