# Patterns — The Minto Pyramid Principle

## Top-Down Build (the box procedure)
**When to use**: whenever you can state, even roughly, the Subject and the reader's Question — the preferred method.
**How**: 1) Draw the top box, write the Subject. 2) Decide the Question. 3) Write the Answer. 4) Identify the Situation (first noncontroversial statement). 5) Develop the Complication ("Yes, I know that, so what?"). 6) Recheck that the Complication triggers exactly your Question. 7) Answer the New Question on the Key Line, deductively or inductively.
**Trade-offs**: fast and reliable when the apex is knowable; useless if you genuinely can't state the top yet.

## Bottom-Up Build
**When to use**: the Main Point is unknown; you only have a pile of points.
**How**: list every point → work out the relationships → draw the conclusion the group implies → restructure into a pyramid and re-check top-down.
**Trade-offs**: rescues a foggy start, but slower and easier to mis-group; always finish by validating top-down.

## SCQA Introduction
**When to use**: the opening of any document.
**How**: tell, in story form, the **Situation** (what the reader accepts) → **Complication** (what changed/went wrong) → **Question** (what they now ask) → **Answer** (your Main Point). Remind, never inform — no proof or exhibits.
**Trade-offs**: frames interpretation powerfully; abused, it becomes manipulation, so keep the Situation genuinely noncontroversial.

## The Four Introduction Patterns
**When to use**: match to the reader's question.
**How**:
- *Giving Direction* (What/How should we do?) — plant the Question: S: want X; C: need you to do Y; Q: How?
- *Seeking Approval* (Should we?) — S: problem; C: solution costs $; Q: approve? Defend with: can't wait / this solves it / cost offset by savings / other goodies.
- *Explaining How-to* — Key Line = Steps; lay out present vs. recommended process; the gap = the steps.
- *Choosing Among Alternatives* — alternatives go in the Complication; Key Line structured by the choice *criteria*.
**Trade-offs**: instant scaffolds, but only fit business/consulting genres; don't force onto pure exposition.

## Summarize a Group (kill the blank assertion)
**When to use**: writing the point above any grouping.
**How**: reword each point as a full sentence → find what's truly alike → for **actions**, state their combined **effect**; for **situations**, state their **implication**. Never "there are N of them."
**Trade-offs**: this is the hardest, highest-value move; rewording often reveals a hidden cause-effect order that reshapes the pyramid.

## Impose Logical Order
**When to use**: ordering any inductive group.
**How**: identify how you formed it — divide a whole → **structural order** (MECE); trace cause-effect → **time order**; classify → **degree order** (importance). If you can't say why point 2 is second, you haven't finished thinking.
**Trade-offs**: only three valid orders; the discipline exposes cause/effect confusions you'd otherwise ship.

## Problem-Definition Framework
**When to use**: any problem-solving document.
**How**: lay out **Situation** (Opening Scene + Disturbing Event) → **R1** (Undesired Result) → **R2** (Desired Result, specific & quantifiable). Identify where the reader stands toward a solution (Minto's seven cases). Read left-to-right-and-down to get the introduction; last thing known = Complication.
**Trade-offs**: converts problem → intro mechanically; a vague R2 makes solution choice impossible, so quantify it.

## Diagnostic Frameworks (hypothesis-driven analysis)
**When to use**: finding the *causes* of a defined problem.
**How**: visualize the problem area, derive likely causes as hypotheses, then gather data only to answer yes/no. Pick one: **show physical structure**, **trace cause-effect** (financial/task/activity tree), or **classify causes** (MECE). Assess causes in order of ease of elimination.
**Trade-offs**: avoids the "collect everything" waste; requires the discipline to test hypotheses rather than admire facts. (Diagnostic frameworks find causes; logic trees generate recommendations — don't conflate.)

## Hierarchical Headings (reflect the pyramid on the page)
**When to use**: laying out a longer document.
**How**: subordinate ideas further right; parallel form within a group; never a lone element; introduce each group; keep concise; the doc must read without them.
**Trade-offs**: makes the ToC a precis — but only if the boxes hold real ideas, not labels like Findings/Conclusions.
