Meridian Pay · 9 July 2026 · Internal — Board / Risk / FCA · single source of record

A settlement outage that was a control failure, not a technology one.

A single expired certificate stopped settlement for 4 hours 11 minutes. But the certificate expiring was a symptom. The real defect is that we had no control that would have caught it, and no path to settle while it was down. The fix is not "renew certificates on time."

The one thing to take away
The fix is three structural changes: an independent settlement fallback, a control that fails the deploy rather than the customer, and a treasury-grade reconciliation that makes financial truth independent of the payments system. Everything below builds to those three.
Situation

Settlement and authorisation are two different systems. That distinction is the whole story.

Complication

Settlement stopped; authorisation kept working, so the incident was invisible on the screen everyone trusts.

Question

What control do we lack, such that one cert can stop settlement, blind treasury, and risk a mis-funded scheme call?

Answer

Three structural controls — owned, dated, costed — that make settlement survivable, safe, and independently true.

Part 1What happened, minute by minute →
Part 1 · the mechanism

The trigger was mundane. The shape of the failure was not.

A TLS client certificate the settlement gateway used to authenticate to the scheme expired at 08:47:03. It had a 397-day life, was issued by an engineer who has since left, and predated the automated rotation. Nothing renewed it; nothing alerted on it. What turned an expiry into a four-hour outage was three compounding failures.

Three things compounded

a
The failure was silent to the layer that mattered.

The gateway logged TLS handshake failures, but the settlement service read a failed connection as "scheme busy, retry later" — the same code path as a transient blip. It queued and retried indefinitely without escalating. The queue grew at ~1,150 transactions/minute; from most dashboards the system looked up.

b
Authorisation stayed green, which hid the bleed.

Authorisation runs on a separate path that doesn't depend on the settlement cert, so the customer-facing "are we up?" status stayed green. The single most-watched number in the company — authorisation success rate — never moved off 99.98%. The incident was invisible on exactly the screen everyone trusts.

c
The queue was mistaken for money.

The treasury dashboard computes balance partly from the settlement service's "in-flight" value. With settlement stalled, in-flight ballooned and the balance widget read it as headroom. At 10:52 a treasury officer opened a ticket asking whether to pre-fund a €19m margin call that was not coming — an artefact of the stall. This is the closest we came to a real loss of funds.

Part 3 · the root cause is a missing control

Not one expired cert — five missing controls.

"A certificate expired" is the trigger, not the cause. We use a simple test: for each candidate cause, if it had been different, would the outage have been prevented or bounded? Five pass that test — and they are not five bugs, but five instances of one pattern.

Five instances of one pattern

1
No inventory of manual certificates.

The expired cert was outside auto-rotation and nothing tracked its expiry.

had it existed → never starts
2
No settlement-throughput alarm.

Zero settlements during trading hours paged no one.

had it existed → detect in minutes
3
Retry logic that hides failure.

"Cert rejected" was treated identically to "network blip", so it retried silently forever.

had it existed → escalates at once
4
No settlement fallback.

Exactly one path to settle; when it is down, we are down.

had it existed → never queues
5
Financial truth derived from the payments system.

Treasury trusted the settlement service's in-flight number, so a stall looked like headroom.

had it existed → €19m artefact impossible

We built for the happy path and trusted the systems that were themselves failing to tell us the truth. The certificate is interchangeable — next time it is a DNS change, a scheme API bump, a bad deploy. The controls are what matter.

The vulnerability, in one config commit

Two latent defects on the same object, committed together a year before they fired. Neither was caught in review because neither did anything — until one expired.

The risk we should have been carrying

There is a standard way to have priced this, and we did not do it. Honestly parameterised before the event, the number justified building the fallback last year.

operational_risk.calcformula · estimate
Expected annual loss ≈ P(trigger fires) × MTTR × cost_rate

  P(some settlement-path fault / yr)  ≈ 0.6   # many un-inventoried manual deps
  MTTR (no alarm, no fallback)        ≈ 4 h   # as we now know
  cost_rate of a settlement stall      ≈ £0.55m / h   # penalties + goodwill

  ⇒ not a rounding error. The three fixes buy this expected loss toward zero.

Part 4 · three structural controls — owned, dated, costed

Monitoring is not the fix. Structure is.

A faster alarm on the same fragile system just tells you sooner that you are down. The fix is to change the structure so the same class of fault cannot stop settlement, cannot ship, and cannot blind treasury.

Control A

Independent settlement fallback

A second, independently-credentialed path to the scheme's backup endpoint, on a separate certificate lifecycle, that the service fails over to automatically when the primary refuses connections for >60 seconds. This turns "we are down" into "customers never notice."

Owner D. Okafor£780k + £120k/yrLive 30 Sep 2026
Control B

Deploys & dependencies fail safe, not silent

(i) A pre-deploy gate that refuses to ship any service whose external deps include a cert expiring within 45 days or a manually-pinned credential not in rotation; and (ii) retry logic that classifies an auth rejection as permanent and escalates within 60s instead of retrying as transient.

Owner R. Silva£240kLive 22 Aug 2026
Control C

Treasury-grade independent reconciliation

A service that computes balance and in-flight value from the scheme's confirmations and the ledger of record — never from the settlement service's own optimistic view. A stall shows up as falling settled volume, not rising headroom; a funding decision can never be made off an artefact.

Owner M. Haas£520k + £90k/yrLive 15 Oct 2026

The remediation plan, at a glance

B ships first (cheapest; stops the next silent failure shipping), A second (biggest customer-facing protection), C third (deepest; the one the regulator cares most about).

The new settlement flow — explicit states, an automatic path out

Today settlement has two effective states: settling and silently-retrying-forever. The redesign has no state in which the system is down and no one is being paged.

From FAILING-OVER, if the fallback also refuses, the system goes to HALTED-AND-PAGING (treasury frozen, incident auto-opened) rather than the old silent retry. There is no state in which the system is down and no one is being paged.

Part 5 · the controls that watch the controls

A plan the board cannot verify is a promise, not a control.

Each fix ships with an explicit, watchable signal, reported to the Risk Committee monthly until each has survived a real trigger.

Time-to-detect a settlement stall

Injected controlled fault in staging monthly; time the page.

today: unbounded (4h11m)target: <5 min
Failover success (Control A)

Fallback must carry 100% of settlement within 90s of a primary failure in the monthly game-day.

target: <90s · 100% of volume
Deploys blocked by the gate (Control B)

We expect it to block some deploys — that is it working. Report the count; confirm none false-blocked into an outage.

target: ≥1 real expiring-cert deploy caught
Reconciliation independence (Control C)

Funding decisions off the settlement service's own view must be zero; balance reconciles to scheme confirmations each 90s cycle.

target: 0 dependent · <£10k recon gap

The regulator-facing summary · FCA operational-resilience file

In the register the regulator expects.

For the operational-resilience file

Meridian Pay experienced a settlement disruption of 4 hours 11 minutes on 9 July 2026, exceeding our stated impact tolerance of 2 hours for the settlement service. Root cause was an expired manually-managed TLS certificate, compounded by the absence of (i) settlement-throughput alerting, (ii) an independent settlement path, and (iii) reconciliation independent of the payments system. No customer funds were lost; all authorised payments settled the same day; measured cost was £2.31m. Three structural controls are owned, costed, and dated (live between 22 August and 15 October 2026), each with a defined, monthly-tested assurance signal. We assess residual risk as materially reduced on delivery of Control A, and as within tolerance on delivery of all three.
