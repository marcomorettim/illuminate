01OPPORTUNITY
Why now, why this audience

Lapsed buyers are already in a buying mindset at peak — reactivating beats reacquiring.

The gifting window is the whole opportunity: a lapsed homeware buyer is shopping for someone else, so the reason to reach back is the season itself, not a discount.

Cheaper than January

Reactivating in November is materially cheaper than reacquiring the same customer in January. The window is time-bound and the economics only hold inside it.

The second purchase

A reactivated customer who buys once in the gifting window has historically come back for a second, self-purchase within eight weeks — so a win-back reactivation is worth more than the first order alone.

Forgetting, not defection

The base lapses because it drifted, not because it left for a competitor — which is why a reminder, not a rescue offer, is the right opening move.

STANCE

Restraint-first, by design

The approach is a ladder, not a blast: open with a reminder, escalate only for non-responders, cap frequency hard, and stop the instant someone converts. A modest incentive is held in reserve for the deepest-lapsed only. The campaign succeeds if it reactivates without burning the list — and that is measured against a proper holdout, never against last year.

02COHORTS
The disagreement the data settled

Two viable cohorts, not three — and the reason is organic baseline.

The strategy memo proposed running all three lapsed cohorts. The analytics readout tested them against value and found only two clear the bar. This is a genuine cross-document conflict — surfaced here, resolved on the data, and already adopted by the creative and the plan.

03LADDER
The journey, the assets, the restraint

A four-rung ladder that stops the instant it works.

Capped at one touch per 72h, max four over six weeks, quiet hours 20:00–08:00, gifting-framed throughout — and every pending message cancels on purchase. The incentive is a single £10, held to the last rung, for the deepest-lapsed Lapsed-Loyal only.

04RULES
The two non-negotiables

Cancel on purchase. Judge only against the holdout.

Two rules recur across the strategy, creative, and plan documents — deduplicated here to one canonical statement each, because they are what keep the campaign honest and non-annoying.

① Cancel-on-purchase

The single most important rule, stated in three documents: on purchase, all pending sends cancel. No customer who has already come back receives another "come back" message.

② Holdout, not raw

A 10% randomised holdout per active cohort receives nothing; the campaign is judged only on lift over that holdout — never on raw reactivation, which would take credit for organic sales.

CODE-CONFIG

The suppression rule, and why it must ship first

cancel_on_purchase.sql
-- Runs before every send. A purchase since enrolment removes every pending message.
UPDATE journey_queue q
SET    q.status = 'CANCELLED'
FROM   orders o
WHERE  o.account_id = q.account_id
  AND  o.placed_at >= q.enrolled_at
  AND  q.status = 'PENDING';   -- reactivated ⇒ silence, immediately
Dependency & risk. The cancellation logic (Ops) must be live and tested before Wave 2, or the incentive wave risks paying £10 to customers who have already reactivated — the one failure mode that turns the campaign margin-negative.
GUARDRAIL

The list-health circuit breaker

Unsubscribe rate must stay under 0.4% per send; if it breaches, the ladder pauses automatically. Reactivating without burning the list is the whole point — a breach means the restraint failed, and the campaign stops rather than pushes.
