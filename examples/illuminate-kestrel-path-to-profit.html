The path to profit is not a growth problem — it is an architecture problem.

Kestrel has proven it can grow: 6.8m customers, €14.2bn deposits, won in eight years. What it has not proven is that a Kestrel customer is worth having — today the average active customer loses the bank €3.20 a month, and growth multiplies that loss rather than diluting it. The fix is not better customers or a trimmed budget line. Each of the four economic engines was built to maximise scale and is therefore structurally loss-making at the unit level. You cannot optimise your way out of a machine built for the wrong objective — you rebuild the machine.

SCQA

The argument in one arc

Situation. The growth thesis is settled — 6.8m customers (4.1m UK, 1.6m DE, 1.1m rest-EU), €14.2bn deposits, €5.1bn loans, 61% active, EU + UK licences. On every top-of-funnel metric Kestrel has won.

Complication. Beneath the growth the unit economics are inverted, and −€3.20 is not improving with scale — it is a property of how the bank is built, so every cohort inherits it. The Series-E is priced on a profitability path, and "more of the same, bigger" is not one.

Question. What has to change so the next customer adds margin instead of subtracting it — structurally and durably, not as a one-quarter cost cut?

Answer. Re-architect the four engines — funding cost, credit quality, cost-to-serve, monetization — each of which owns exactly one term of the unit-economics identity.

01FUNDING COST
Net interest margin · +€4.10 → +€5.60 · +€1.50 / customer / mo

The balance sheet was built to grow deposits, not to fund cheaply.

Pull the blended cost of funds from 2.8% to 1.9% — 90bp across the full €14.2bn base, worth ~€128m/yr and ~€1.50/customer/month. But not by paying depositors less: the balances were bought with the 3.9% promo rate and leave if it lapses without a substitute. The 90bp comes from re-architecting which liabilities Kestrel holds, how long they stay, and how they behave when rates move.

Driver 1 · mix

"€14.2bn of deposits" is a fiction of aggregation

It is four instruments priced 0.45%–4.40% with behavioural lives from 4 months to 4.5 years. Read the table as a target list: two rows — promotional + treasury-placed, 48% of the book — supply 1.92 of the 2.80 points of cost while contributing the least franchise value.

Reconciles exactly: 0.38×3.90 + 0.29×2.70 + 0.23×0.45 + 0.10×4.40 = 2.80%. Mechanisms: retire the promotional marginal euro (shed ~€2.27bn of the hottest money, accept 2–3 quarters of negative deposit growth — the point, not the failure ); migrate rate-chasers into salaried primary relationships (~35% take-up, €40–60/migration, recovered on the E4 line ); reprice treasury to the institutional curve (−14bp of blended cost).

Drivers 2–4 · the chain

Mix → duration → rate-sensitivity → placement

D2 · Stability

A cheap deposit that leaves in 90 days is expensive: unstable money forces punitive HQLA. Re-architecting the mix releases ~€288m of HQLA from a ~3.2% holding pen into placements earning ~100bp more — ~€2.9m/yr from the same deposits behaving predictably. Salary anchoring converts a 10% (less-stable) runoff into 5% (stable).

D3 · Rate sensitivity

€14.2bn deposits vs ~€7.6bn rate-sensitive assets → liability-sensitive: +200bp costs −€0.34/customer/mo, and beta asymmetry (up 0.85 vs down 0.40) means the book loses in both directions. Cutting blended β from 0.58 → 0.45 shrinks the up-200bp drag from −€27.9m to ~−€10m/yr.

D4 · Placement & yield

NIM is the spread, and the spread is only as good as where the money sits. Deploy the released HQLA at ~4.2%, term out placement now that duration is matched — and, decisively, fund better credit, not more credit: a book funded at 1.9% can profitably hold prime loans a 2.8% book could not. This is the funding↔credit coupling.

What counts

The one decisive lever

Not the promotional rate, not the HQLA yield — it is deposit mix, and specifically the deliberate migration of rate-chasing balances into salaried, primary-banking relationships. Mix sets duration (D2), duration sets the LCR runoff that frees the balance sheet, freedom lowers beta (D3), and a low-beta stable cheap book is the precondition for terming out and holding better credit (D4). Kestrel cannot fund cheaply by paying less — only by holding different, stickier liabilities. Shrink the promotional book on purpose; convert what you can into primary relationships; let stability — not price — buy the 90bp. deposit mix

02CREDIT QUALITY
Expected credit loss · −€5.85 → −€2.65 · +€3.20 — the largest recoverable term

A book underwritten for approval rates, not loss rates.

Take the loss rate on the €5.1bn book from 3.4% to 2.1% — a €3.20 swing that alone crosses the identity to breakeven. Not by lending less: the 1.3 points of excess loss are the residue of a machine optimised to maximise approvals. We recover them by re-underwriting how the book decides, holds, collects, and provisions.

BNPL is 19% of the book but ~41% of euro losses, yielding 4.1% against a 7.8% loss — held because it acquired customers, not because it earned. Cutting the 2.6%-loss overdraft would destroy 19.4% yield to save a loss the yield pays many times over.

Driver 2 · the scorecard

The objective function was approval, not loss

The model ranks well but is thresholded to volume — under a growth mandate the cut-off drifts down every quarter. The re-architected scorer adds cash-flow features from Kestrel's own deposit rails (a bureau can't see them); the strongest single lift is gambling_merchant_ratio — more than a 90-point bureau swing. Back-test Gini 0.61 vs 0.48 bureau-only, concentrated in the thin-file tail.

origination_score_v3.sql
-- rank-order 12-month default probability, NOT approval rate
SELECT application_id, product_line, geo,
  -- logit calibrated to observed 12m default (vintage fit):
  1.0 / (1.0 + EXP(-(
      -3.10                                  -- intercept
    - 0.0042 * (COALESCE(bureau_score,620)-620)
    + 0.55  * thin_file::int            -- thin-file penalty
    - 0.31  * LEAST(salary_credits_12m,12) -- regular income protects
    + 1.90  * gambling_merchant_ratio     -- strongest single lift
    + 0.80  * balance_volatility
    + 0.34  * (geo = 'REST_EU')::int    -- residual geo skew → Driver 4
  ))) AS pd_12m
FROM features;

Re-threshold from "approve to the loss ceiling" → "approve to where marginal loss-adjusted yield turns negative," per product per geo. Declines the bottom decile of rest-EU while opening thin-file UK the bureau wrongly declined — loss-neutral on volume (−0.4%), −0.6pt modelled loss. This is where funding↔credit bites: cheaper funding widens the loss-adjusted-yield floor.

Driver 3 · roll-rates

Loss is a flow, and the flow is arrestable

The leverage is overwhelmingly at the 1–29 → 30–59 gate, where a cure is cheapest — before a missed payment hardens. The automation is built and paid for inside E3 (cost-to-serve), yet the loss it prevents is booked in E2: the same event lowers two identity terms.

Driver 4 · IFRS-9

Reconcile the cash loss with the booked loss

Cured-but-restructured loans sit in Stage 2 carrying a lifetime provision even as cash normalises — so aggressive collections can raise booked ECL in the quarter it lowers cash loss. A disciplined cure-to-Stage-1 policy releases provision as roll-rates season. And the 1.7× rest-EU skew must be provisioned explicitly, not averaged: geo-segmented ECL releases 0.4pt of UK over-coverage while adding 0.9pt to rest-EU — net flat, but honestly located, and the rest-EU signal is exactly what drove the origination cut-off.

What counts

One lever is decisive

The scorecard's objective function. Fix the mix without it and the threshold drifts back within a year; automate collections without it and you cure a book origination keeps re-poisoning; segment the provision without it and you only measure a loss you keep choosing to underwrite. Re-specify origination from maximise approvals subject to a loss ceiling to maximise loss-adjusted yield per product per geography — and the other three drivers become durable. Everything else in Engine 2 is the machinery this one decision makes worth building. the scorecard objective

03COST-TO-SERVE
Cost-to-serve · −€4.85 → −€2.85 · €58 → €34 / yr · a 41% cut, ~€163m annualised

An operating model that scaled headcount with customers.

The problem isn't cost-per-contact; it's that cost is linear in customers — cost = customers × human_touches × cost_per_touch, every term rising together. Re-architecting means breaking the linearity: make the marginal customer consume near-zero human time, so the curve bends from linear toward flat.

The bridge

The last line moves not at all — scheme and statutory cost is not an architecture problem, and pretending otherwise would be the dial-turning the thesis forbids. The €24 is earned in the first four rows (dark = target, yellow = the biggest single prize: contact operations). Contact begins the chain.

Drivers 1–2

Deflection, then root-cause suppression, then ML triage

A live call costs ~100× a self-serve answer. Shift the funnel from 40/20/40 to 55/33/12 (self-serve / bot / agent) and blended cost/intent falls €2.14 → €0.71. The LLM assistant gets scoped write access — freeze a card, dispute a charge — so it resolves rather than deflects-and-frustrates. The 12% agent floor is deliberate: ~8–10% of intents (bereavement, fraud distress, FCA-mandated) are non-deflectable; a target below ~11% is a red flag, not an achievement.

Cheaper still is the contact never formed: real-time payment status, plain-language decline reasons, and enriched merchant names suppress ~18% of intent at source — and enriched naming crosses into E2, cutting first-party "I don't recognise this charge" disputes. Fraud/disputes get an ML triage layer: only the ambiguous ~22% band reaches an analyst (−65% touched volume), with asymmetric thresholds (auto-block bar higher than auto-clear) and geo-conditioning for the 1.7× rest-EU rate.

What counts

Break the linearity, or re-inflate next quarter

None of it holds unless the event→automation→human-fallback pattern with bounded, learning authority becomes the default path for every servicing task — marginal customer at near-zero human time, a deliberate human floor protecting what must stay human. Deflection percentages and cloud bills are downstream of that one decision. Bend the cost curve from linear toward flat — the only version of €34 that survives the next 3 million customers. bounded automation

What counts

The subscription re-architecture — paid take-rate ~9% → ~18%, weighted to Metal. Interchange is frozen, treasury is macro-exposed and shared with E1, cross-sell is margin-thin and credit-encumbered. Subscriptions are the only lever uncapped, recurring, high-margin, wholly in Kestrel's control — and that simultaneously reduces the cost-to-serve it charges for. Synthesis · how four engines sum to a bank that works

Four disciplined moves, sequenced so the couplings help.

The engines were argued separately; they do not act separately. The €9.30 swing is assembled from the four terms in an order that matters — because each engine's gain partly depends on another's.

The one dependency

What the whole plan hangs on

Sequence: Credit (E2) first — it crosses zero and can't be parallelised away. Funding (E1) second (unlocks E2's safer book). Monetization (E4) third (funds E3). Cost-to-serve (E3) fourth in the P&L but started in parallel — because its collections-automation platform must be live before E2's re-underwriting can hit its loss target. Sequence that wrong and Engine 2 delivers two-thirds of its number while collections cost overruns and the breakeven step fails. Everything else can slip a quarter; this cannot. The one decision that determines the outcome: fund this as one architecture programme — four coupled engines rebuilt together — or as four cost-cutting projects handed to four owners. The first reaches +€6.10 and holds; the second turns dials, books a good quarter, and leaves the machine as loss-making as today.
