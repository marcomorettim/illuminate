Rill · Second Wind · dormant-user reactivation

Around 1.4M Rill users have quietly gone dormant — no app opens, auto-save paused, the round-up habit lapsed. There is no cancellation event to react to, only a slow fade. This brief is not an argument about whether to win them back. It is the machine that does it.

The campaign has to earn the re-open, not demand it.

Because the dormant base is a fragile asset — people who granted an app permission, a phone number, a bank link — a generic "we miss you" is the fastest way to turn a dormant-but-retained user into an opt-out. So Second Wind is engineered as a system: segment the base, climb a restraint-first ladder, orchestrate one guardrailed journey, and measure everything against a holdout.

The system at a glance

One journey, five subsystems, wrapped in guardrails.

Second Wind is a single Journey Builder journey. Dormant users are scored into cohorts nightly, split off a 10% holdout, routed to a cohort track, escalated up the ladder across channels, and either reactivate — at which point every pending send is cancelled — or fall through to an evergreen Winback. Subsystem 1 · Segmentation

The dormant base is not one audience. It is four.

Every dormant user lands in exactly one of four mutually-exclusive cohorts, scored nightly and re-checked at every step against the live data extension. Each cohort stopped for a different reason, so each needs a different reason to come back — and a different answer to the incentive question.

Subsystem 2 · The messaging ladder

Escalate only as far as each user needs.

The creative strategy is one four-rung spine for everyone; the words change by cohort. Most reactivations should happen on the lower rungs — the incentive is the last resort, not the opener. Cohort 4 never sees rung 4.

1Day 0

Reconnect — show them the pot

The first touch is a mirror, not a pitch: here is the value that accrued while you were away. For funded cohorts, a real balance — "You've saved £142 without noticing." Concreteness beats sentiment; a number they recognise outperforms "we miss you."

Subsystem 3 · The journey

Trigger-based orchestration — the operational core.

A single journey with a nightly trigger entry and cohort-driven decision splits. The Funded-Then-Lapsed track is shown as the reference; others vary by copy, cadence and incentive eligibility. Every wait re-checks eligibility against live data.

On entry · split
Holdout split — 10% Control

A deterministic 10% of entrants are routed to a Control path that sends nothing and exits after 30 days. It is what makes the headline number incremental, not gross.

Orchestration rules — every step

  • Channel priority: Push › SMS › Email, gated by permission; in-app cards are additive and permission-free.
  • Frequency cap: max 1 send / 48h, max 4 across the journey (Cohort 4 capped at 3).
  • Quiet hours: no push/SMS 21:00–08:00 local; sends queue to the next window.
  • Global governance: a security or servicing message from another journey is prioritised; Second Wind defers.

Suppression on success

The single most important rule, and the highest-risk bug at soft launch: the moment a reactivation event fires — auto-save resumed, recurring transfer created, or a qualifying deposit — all pending sends are cancelled and the user exits to Success.

Messaging someone the day after they already came back is the classic reactivation failure. Stale segmentation is why every step re-checks the live data extension rather than trusting entry-time state.

Subsystem 4 · The creative

What actually lands on the phone.

The ladder rendered as real assets, personalized via %%Field%% tokens resolved at send time — every dynamic value resolves from the record or falls back to a safe default; no value is invented at render. These are faithful depictions of the brief's copy, not live sends — tap a pin for the source.

The reactivation screen every message points to is not a generic home screen — it's a purpose-built "here's where you stood, here's the one thing to do" surface, closing with the line that does the real work: "Your money stayed exactly where you left it. Nothing changed while you were away."

Incrementality via holdout

The 10% control receives nothing. The reported lift is targeted-reactivation minus holdout-reactivation — the number that goes to the business. Gross reactivation flatters the campaign by counting organic returns that would have happened anyway. The holdout is non-negotiable.

Retention read 30 / 60 / 90 days. A cohort that reactivates at 10% but re-lapses to 3% active by day 60 is worse than one that reactivates at 7% and holds 6%.

Experiment matrix (v1)

  • Test A — incentive vs value-only (Day-10, Cohorts 1&2): does the £5 boost produce incremental reactivations net of the holdout? Kill it if it merely subsidises would-have-returned users.
  • Test B — reach: push-only vs push+SMS. Does the SMS fallback lift Reached→Reactivated enough to justify send cost and opt-out risk?
  • Both read against the shared holdout, with pre-registered stopping rules and an MDE agreed with Data before launch.
Subsystem 6 · Guardrails & compliance

The caps matter as much as the target.

The dormant base is an asset; the campaign is allowed to spend a small, measured amount of its goodwill — and the guardrails cap the spend. A win on the primary KPI does not count if any guardrail breaches, and a breaching arm pauses automatically.

App uninstall among messaged users must not be statistically above the holdout.

The compliance box — tighter than retail

  • Reach is the binding constraint, not creative: push covers only ~45% of the base, SMS ~30% consented, email the rest — which raises cost and opt-out exposure.
  • Consent is a hard gate: reactivating a lapsed user is not a lawful basis to message a number that never consented. STOP/HELP honoured instantly and permanently.
  • Financial-promotion rules: fair, clear, not misleading; no guaranteed returns; capital-at-risk where micro-invest is referenced; "saving" and "investing" never used interchangeably.
  • The risk is asymmetric: a lost opt-out is harder to win back than a missed reactivation — which is why restraint, not reach, is the default.

Held open · not yet decided

What the brief deliberately leaves unresolved.

Four questions the brief names as open — carried honestly rather than answered prematurely.

Q1Should Withdrew-Partial receive any reactivation messaging in v1?›
Or should Cohort 4 be held for a dedicated trust-rebuilding track later? The brief leans toward a softer cadence and no incentive, but whether they should be messaged at all is unsettled.
Q2Is a £5 boost the right incentive magnitude?›
Or does a match-style mechanic — "we'll match your first restart save up to £X" — reactivate more durably? Test A will read incrementality, but the mechanic itself is open.
Q3What is the right handoff to evergreen Winback?›
One touch a month, or quarterly — before a dormant user should simply be left alone? The 30-day journey ends; the long tail's cadence is undecided.
Q4Do involuntary lapses need their own register?›
Should users whose dormancy coincides with a failed payment (an involuntary lapse) get a different creative register from those who chose to pause? The brief flags the distinction but doesn't yet split on it.
